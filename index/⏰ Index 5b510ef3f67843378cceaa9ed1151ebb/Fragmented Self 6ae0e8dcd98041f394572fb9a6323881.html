Steppenwolf’s Relationship with the Immortals

It is love for and devotion to some great man, Nietzsche writes, that alone “can bestow on the soul, not only a clear, discriminating self-contemptuous view of itself, but also the desire to look beyond itself and to seek with all its might for a higher self as yet concealed from it” (SE 6, p. 163). (Franco 57)
Our unique self is not something buried deep within us, waiting to be uncovered through introspection; rather, it is something that can be disclosed only by attaching ourselves to great human beings who make us ashamed of our lazy conformity to convention and public opinion and inspire us to transfigure our lives in accordance with our higher selves. (Franco 57)

Multiplicity at the core (no unified self, intellect or rationality that integrates and harmonizes)

All of this is compatible with the traditional picture of self-mastery. But where Nietzsche departs from this picture is in denying that the very desire to combat a drive lies within our power or emanates from some unified self or reason: “in this entire procedure our intellect is only the blind instrument of another drive which is a rival of the drive whose vehemence is tormenting us” (D 109). It is not a matter of reason supervening on passion but of one drive complaining about another. The self does not conflict with the drives but is merely the site where the drives themselves conflict. (Franco 61)

Music & Self-creation

Of Beethoven, Mozart, and Wagner’s music:

…by means of their weeknesses they have all produced in us a ravenous hunger for their virtues and a ten times more sensitive palate for every drop of musical spirit, musical beauty, musical goodness. (D 218)
To “give style” to one’s characters…[one must] survey all the strengths and weaknesses of [one’s] nature and then fit them into an artistic plan until every one of them appears as art and reason and even weaknesses delight the eye.” (GS 290)

We can rearrange the ugly so that it can be “reinterpreted and made sublime.” What matters is that a single taste governs. “In the aphorism (299 GS) on what one can learn from artists, Nietzsche again stresses how artists work with what they are given, even if it is ugly, transforming it rather than simply denying it…The only problem with artists, Nietzsche observes, is that they restrict their use of these techniques to their art rather than applying them to their lives. This is where he insists we must go beyond them, for “we want to be the poets of our life” (GS 299). (Franco 70)

“It is with a view to attaining satisfaction with oneself, taking pleasure in oneself, having a good conscience toward oneself—and not the achievement of aesthetic unity for its own sake—that Nietzsche generally invokesthe analogy of art for self-making” (Franco 70) See GS 299.

Only artists, especially those of theatre, have given men eyes and ears to see and hear with some pleasure what each man is himself, experiences himself, desires himself; only they have taught us to esteem the hero that is concealed in everyday characters; only they have taught us the art of viewing ourselves as heroes—from a distanceand, as it were simplified and transfigured—the art of staging and watching ourselves…Without this art we would be nothing but foreground and live entirley in the spell of that perspective which makes what is closest at hand and most vulgar appear as if it were vast, and reality itself. ” (GS 78).
